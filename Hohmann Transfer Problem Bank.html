Overview

Why this problem matters

The Hohmann transfer is one of the most important orbit-transfer ideas in astrodynamics. Given two circular coplanar orbits, the task is to determine the transfer trajectory, the required \( \Delta v \), and the transfer time.

But this is not only a calculation exercise. It is also a compact example of how assumptions shape a model, how that model leads to equations, and how the resulting solution must still be interpreted in engineering terms.

Problem setup

Mission question

Start with a spacecraft in a circular orbit of radius \( r_1 \). Move it to another circular orbit of radius \( r_2 \) using two impulsive burns.

  • First burn: inject from the initial circular orbit onto a transfer ellipse.
  • Second burn: circularize at the final orbit.

The objective is to compute:

  • the transfer-orbit geometry,
  • the two burn magnitudes \( \Delta v_1 \) and \( \Delta v_2 \),
  • the total maneuver cost \( \Delta v_{\text{total}} \),
  • and the transfer time.
Assumptions

What makes the Hohmann transfer valid?

The usual result is only valid under a specific idealized framework:

  • Circular initial and final orbits
  • Coplanar motion
  • Impulsive burns
  • Two-body dynamics
  • No perturbations or operational constraints
Important: The Hohmann transfer is \( \Delta v \)-optimal only inside this simplified model. Once you include plane changes, low thrust, drag, timing, or operational limits, the answer may change.
Mathematical model

Constructing the equations

Let \( \mu \) be the gravitational parameter of the central body. The transfer ellipse connects the two circular orbits tangentially.

Transfer ellipse semi-major axis

\[ a_t = \frac{r_1 + r_2}{2} \]

Circular orbit velocity

\[ v_c = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{r}} \]

Vis-viva equation

\[ v = \sqrt{\mu\left(\frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a}\right)} \]

On the transfer ellipse, the key velocities are:

  • \( v_{t1} \): transfer velocity at perigee
  • \( v_{t2} \): transfer velocity at apogee

Burn definitions

\[ \Delta v_1 = v_{t1} - v_{c1} \] \[ \Delta v_2 = v_{c2} - v_{t2} \] \[ \Delta v_{\text{total}} = \Delta v_1 + \Delta v_2 \]

Transfer time

\[ t = \pi \sqrt{\frac{a_t^3}{\mu}} \]
Solution logic

How the calculation unfolds

  1. Compute the initial circular velocity \( v_{c1} \).
  2. Compute the transfer velocity at perigee \( v_{t1} \).
  3. Compute the first burn \( \Delta v_1 \).
  4. Compute the transfer velocity at apogee \( v_{t2} \).
  5. Compute the final circular velocity \( v_{c2} \).
  6. Compute the second burn \( \Delta v_2 \).
  7. Sum the burns to obtain total \( \Delta v \).
  8. Compute transfer time from half the transfer-ellipse period.

This example makes the energy interpretation much clearer: reaching GEO is not just “going higher.” It is a major shift to a much higher orbital-energy state.

Python snippet (1): Reusable Hohmann transfer function

General implementation

This is the cleanest reusable implementation for mission studies, calculators, or teaching workflows.

▼ Open: reusable solver 
import math

def hohmann_transfer(mu, r1, r2):
    a_t = 0.5 * (r1 + r2)

    v_c1 = math.sqrt(mu / r1)
    v_c2 = math.sqrt(mu / r2)
    v_t1 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r1 - 1 / a_t))
    v_t2 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r2 - 1 / a_t))

    delta_v1 = abs(v_t1 - v_c1)
    delta_v2 = abs(v_c2 - v_t2)
    delta_v_total = delta_v1 + delta_v2
    transfer_time = math.pi * math.sqrt(a_t**3 / mu)

    return {
        "a_t": a_t,
        "v_c1": v_c1,
        "v_t1": v_t1,
        "delta_v1": delta_v1,
        "v_t2": v_t2,
        "v_c2": v_c2,
        "delta_v2": delta_v2,
        "delta_v_total": delta_v_total,
        "transfer_time": transfer_time,
    }

import math

mu = 3.986004418e14
R_earth = 6378e3

def hohmann_transfer(mu, r1, r2):
    a_t = 0.5 * (r1 + r2)
    v_c1 = math.sqrt(mu / r1)
    v_c2 = math.sqrt(mu / r2)
    v_t1 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r1 - 1 / a_t))
    v_t2 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r2 - 1 / a_t))
    delta_v1 = abs(v_t1 - v_c1)
    delta_v2 = abs(v_c2 - v_t2)
    delta_v_total = delta_v1 + delta_v2
    transfer_time = math.pi * math.sqrt(a_t**3 / mu)

    return delta_v1, delta_v2, delta_v_total, transfer_time

# Case 1: 300 km to 1000 km
case1 = hohmann_transfer(mu, R_earth + 300e3, R_earth + 1000e3)
print("300 km to 1000 km:", case1)

# Case 2: 300 km to GEO
case2 = hohmann_transfer(mu, R_earth + 300e3, 42164e3)
print("300 km to GEO:", case2)
Engineering insight

Why this problem is more than a textbook formula

The Hohmann transfer is powerful because it connects physical reasoning, mathematical structure, and implementation in a very compact example.

  • It shows when a simple model is useful.
  • It shows how \( \Delta v \) reflects orbital energy change, not just “speed added.”
  • It reveals why two problems with the same equations can have very different engineering meaning.
Final takeaway: For modest orbit raising, the maneuver is small and intuitive. For Earth-to-GEO transfer, the same equations reveal a much larger energy transition. The mathematics is identical, but the mission interpretation is very different.

That is exactly why this belongs in a Problem Bank: it teaches not only how to calculate, but how to reason through transfer design.
