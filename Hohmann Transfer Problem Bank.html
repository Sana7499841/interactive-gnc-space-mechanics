Overview

Why this problem matters

The Hohmann transfer is one of the most important orbit-transfer ideas in astrodynamics. Given two circular coplanar orbits, the task is to determine the transfer trajectory, the required \( \Delta v \), and the transfer time.

But this is not only a calculation exercise. It is also a compact example of how assumptions shape a model, how that model leads to equations, and how the resulting solution must still be interpreted in engineering terms.

The page is developed as a structured engineering walkthrough: Problem → Roadmap → Assumptions → Model → Solution → Examples → Code → Insight.

Problem setup

Mission question

Start with a spacecraft in a circular orbit of radius \( r_1 \). Move it to another circular orbit of radius \( r_2 \) using two impulsive burns.

  • First burn: inject from the initial circular orbit onto a transfer ellipse.
  • Second burn: circularize at the final orbit.

The objective is to compute:

  • the transfer-orbit geometry,
  • the two burn magnitudes \( \Delta v_1 \) and \( \Delta v_2 \),
  • the total maneuver cost \( \Delta v_{\text{total}} \),
  • and the transfer time.

Assumptions

What makes the Hohmann transfer valid?

The usual result is only valid under a specific idealized framework:

  • Circular initial and final orbits
  • Coplanar motion
  • Impulsive burns
  • Two-body dynamics
  • No perturbations or operational constraints
Important: The Hohmann transfer is \( \Delta v \)-optimal only inside this simplified model. Once you include plane changes, low thrust, drag, timing, or operational limits, the answer may change.
Mathematical model

Constructing the equations

Let \( \mu \) be the gravitational parameter of the central body. The transfer ellipse connects the two circular orbits tangentially.

Transfer ellipse semi-major axis

\[ a_t = \frac{r_1 + r_2}{2} \]

Circular orbit velocity

\[ v_c = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{r}} \]

Vis-viva equation

\[ v = \sqrt{\mu\left(\frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a}\right)} \]

Using the vis-viva equation, we compute the transfer-ellipse velocity at the two critical points:

  • At perigee: velocity just after the first burn
  • At apogee: velocity just before the second burn

Burn definitions

\[ \Delta v_1 = v_{t1} - v_{c1} \] \[ \Delta v_2 = v_{c2} - v_{t2} \] \[ \Delta v_{\text{total}} = \Delta v_1 + \Delta v_2 \]

Transfer time

\[ t = \pi \sqrt{\frac{a_t^3}{\mu}} \]
Solution strategy

How the solution unfolds

The calculation follows the physical sequence of spacecraft motion.

  1. Compute the velocity in the initial circular orbit.
  2. Compute the transfer-orbit velocity at perigee.
  3. Determine the first burn \( \Delta v_1 \).
  4. Compute the transfer-orbit velocity at apogee.
  5. Compute the velocity in the final circular orbit.
  6. Determine the second burn \( \Delta v_2 \).
  7. Sum the burns to obtain the total \( \Delta v \).
  8. Compute the transfer time using half the transfer-ellipse period.

The key idea is that this is a two-step orbital energy adjustment, not merely a geometric change in altitude.

Worked example

Example 1: LEO to a higher circular orbit

We first apply the model to a moderate orbit-raising case: from a 300 km circular low Earth orbit to a 1000 km circular orbit.

Initial orbit

300 km circular LEO

Final orbit

1000 km circular orbit

Earth radius

\( R_E = 6378 \,\text{km} \)

Gravitational parameter

\( \mu = 3.986004418 \times 10^{14} \,\text{m}^3/\text{s}^2 \)

Derived orbit radii

\[ r_1 = 6678 \,\text{km}, \qquad r_2 = 7378 \,\text{km}, \qquad a_t = \frac{6678 + 7378}{2} = 7028 \,\text{km} \]

Step-by-step results

Quantity Value Meaning
Initial circular velocity, \(v_{c1}\) 7725.84 m/s Velocity in the 300 km circular orbit
Transfer velocity at perigee, \(v_{t1}\) 7915.88 m/s Velocity immediately after the first burn
First burn, \( \Delta v_1 \) 190.04 m/s Injection into the transfer ellipse
Transfer velocity at apogee, \(v_{t2}\) 7164.85 m/s Velocity on the transfer ellipse at the higher orbit radius
Final circular velocity, \(v_{c2}\) 7350.21 m/s Required circular velocity at 1000 km altitude
Second burn, \( \Delta v_2 \) 185.36 m/s Circularization at the higher orbit
Total \( \Delta v \) 375.40 m/s Total maneuver cost
Transfer time 48.86 minutes Half the period of the transfer ellipse

In physical terms, the first burn raises orbital energy by entering the transfer ellipse, while the second burn adds the remaining energy needed to circularize at the higher orbit.

▼ Python snippet: LEO orbit-raising case 
import math

# Constants
mu = 3.986004418e14
R_earth = 6378e3

# Orbit radii
r1 = R_earth + 300e3
r2 = R_earth + 1000e3

# Transfer ellipse semi-major axis
a_t = 0.5 * (r1 + r2)

# Velocities
v_c1 = math.sqrt(mu / r1)
v_c2 = math.sqrt(mu / r2)
v_t1 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r1 - 1 / a_t))
v_t2 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r2 - 1 / a_t))

# Delta-v
delta_v1 = v_t1 - v_c1
delta_v2 = v_c2 - v_t2
delta_v_total = delta_v1 + delta_v2

# Transfer time
transfer_time = math.pi * math.sqrt(a_t**3 / mu)

print(f"Initial circular velocity   = {v_c1:.2f} m/s")
print(f"Transfer velocity at r1     = {v_t1:.2f} m/s")
print(f"First burn Δv1              = {delta_v1:.2f} m/s")
print(f"Transfer velocity at r2     = {v_t2:.2f} m/s")
print(f"Final circular velocity     = {v_c2:.2f} m/s")
print(f"Second burn Δv2             = {delta_v2:.2f} m/s")
print(f"Total Δv                    = {delta_v_total:.2f} m/s")
print(f"Transfer time               = {transfer_time/60:.2f} min")

Reusable implementation and combined runner

The two example-specific snippets above are good for teaching. The following versions are cleaner for reuse and comparison.

Reusable Hohmann transfer function

▼ Open: general reusable function 
import math

def hohmann_transfer(mu, r1, r2):
    """
    Compute the Hohmann transfer between two circular coplanar orbits.
    """

    a_t = 0.5 * (r1 + r2)

    v_c1 = math.sqrt(mu / r1)
    v_c2 = math.sqrt(mu / r2)

    v_t1 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r1 - 1 / a_t))
    v_t2 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r2 - 1 / a_t))

    delta_v1 = abs(v_t1 - v_c1)
    delta_v2 = abs(v_c2 - v_t2)
    delta_v_total = delta_v1 + delta_v2

    transfer_time = math.pi * math.sqrt(a_t**3 / mu)

    return {
        "a_t": a_t,
        "v_c1": v_c1,
        "v_t1": v_t1,
        "delta_v1": delta_v1,
        "v_t2": v_t2,
        "v_c2": v_c2,
        "delta_v2": delta_v2,
        "delta_v_total": delta_v_total,
        "transfer_time": transfer_time,
    }

import math

def hohmann_transfer(mu, r1, r2):
    a_t = 0.5 * (r1 + r2)

    v_c1 = math.sqrt(mu / r1)
    v_c2 = math.sqrt(mu / r2)
    v_t1 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r1 - 1 / a_t))
    v_t2 = math.sqrt(mu * (2 / r2 - 1 / a_t))

    delta_v1 = abs(v_t1 - v_c1)
    delta_v2 = abs(v_c2 - v_t2)
    delta_v_total = delta_v1 + delta_v2
    transfer_time = math.pi * math.sqrt(a_t**3 / mu)

    return {
        "a_t_km": a_t / 1000,
        "v_c1": v_c1,
        "v_t1": v_t1,
        "delta_v1": delta_v1,
        "v_t2": v_t2,
        "v_c2": v_c2,
        "delta_v2": delta_v2,
        "delta_v_total": delta_v_total,
        "transfer_time_min": transfer_time / 60,
        "transfer_time_hr": transfer_time / 3600,
    }

Beyond the equation

Why the Hohmann transfer is powerful — and limited

The Hohmann transfer is often described as the optimal solution, but that statement is only true inside the assumptions stated earlier.

  • It is \( \Delta v \)-optimal only for impulsive, coplanar, two-body motion.
  • Low-thrust propulsion does not follow the same two-burn structure. Electric propulsion may spiral outward gradually instead.
  • Plane changes can dominate mission cost. Inclination change may matter more than simple radius change.
  • Mission design is not only about minimum \( \Delta v \). Time, margins, eclipse conditions, communication windows, and propulsion capability also matter.

That is why this topic deserves more than a formula box. It is a small but very useful example of where analytical optimality and real mission design begin to separate.

Engineering insight

Final takeaway

The Hohmann transfer is more than a standard derivation. It is a compact example of engineering reasoning in astrodynamics.

It shows how assumptions shape a model, how a model produces a solution, how that solution can be encoded in Python, and how the final answer must still be interpreted in the context of mission design.

For modest orbit-raising, such as 300 km to 1000 km, the maneuver cost is relatively small and intuitive. For Earth parking orbit to GEO, the same framework reveals a much more demanding energy transition. The mathematics is the same — but the engineering meaning becomes very different.

Key message: this problem does not merely teach a formula. It teaches how to think through orbital transfer design.
